## 🔮 EMERGING BUZZ (From VC Land)

**AI Agents** (Score: 37.5) - Sequoia + 15 YC companies
The term that's absolutely dominating VC conversations right now. But here's what's different from last year's "AI assistant" buzz - we're seeing much more specific language around autonomous execution, not just chat interfaces.

**Mike's take:** This is the real deal, folks. When 15 YC companies are using the same terminology and Sequoia is amplifying it, we're looking at a category that's about to explode. The shift from "AI tools" to "AI agents" signals actual automation that can complete tasks without human handholding. Think less Clippy, more autonomous junior analyst.

**Domain patterns to watch:** The compound opportunities here are massive. Look for patterns like "AgentPlatform," "EnterpriseAgent," "CodeAgent" variations. The beauty is most companies will want the [Industry]Agent.com format - LegalAgent, SalesAgent, DevAgent, etc. This follows the same pattern we saw with "Cloud" prefixes in 2018-2020.

**Coding Agents** (Score: 30.5) - Sequoia + 1 YC company
This is the most interesting sub-category. We're talking about AI that actually writes, reviews, and deploys code autonomously. Not GitHub Copilot autocomplete - actual agents that can take requirements and ship features.

**Mike's take:** If you've been watching the enterprise software space like I have, you know the biggest constraint is always engineering bandwidth. Companies are spending $200K+ per senior engineer, and delivery timelines are still measured in quarters, not weeks. Coding agents that can actually deliver working software? That's a $100B+ market opportunity.

**Domain patterns:** This is where the enterprise naming conventions get interesting. "DevAgent," "CodeAgent," "EngineerAgent" - but also think about the workflow-specific terms: "DeployAgent," "ReviewAgent," "TestAgent." The companies that win here will likely want the authoritative .com in their specific vertical.

**Multi-Agent Systems** (Score: 30.5) - Sequoia + 1 YC company
Now we're getting into the really sophisticated territory. This isn't one AI doing one task - it's orchestrated systems of specialized agents working together.

**Mike's take:** This is where things get enterprise-interesting. I've seen enough Salesforce and Workday implementations to know that Fortune 500 companies don't want point solutions - they want platforms that can handle complex, multi-step business processes. Multi-agent systems are how you get from "cool AI demo" to "replaces three departments."

**Legal AI** (Score: 30.5) - Sequoia + 1 YC company
Legal is always a fascinating vertical to watch because it's both highly regulated and desperately inefficient. When VCs start talking about legal AI, it usually means someone's figured out how to navigate the compliance minefield.

**Mike's take:** Legal tech has been "about to transform" for a decade, but most solutions hit the wall of "lawyers don't trust automation." The fact that this is showing up in VC discussions suggests we might finally have solutions that augment rather than replace legal judgment. Think contract review, discovery automation, regulatory compliance checking.

## 🎯 MIKE'S WEEKLY THESIS

Here's the pattern I'm seeing across all this data: **We're witnessing the transition from AI experimentation to AI operations.**

The VC terminology has shifted from broad concepts like "artificial intelligence" to specific implementation patterns like "coding agents" and "multi-agent systems." Meanwhile, mainstream corporate is talking about the infrastructure required to actually deploy this stuff at scale ("data centers," "AI chips").

This is exactly the pattern we saw with cloud computing in 2017-2019. First the VCs funded the tools and platforms, then the enterprises figured out how to actually implement them, and finally the infrastructure scaled to support mainstream adoption.

**Strategic implications for domain investors:**

1. **Agent-based naming is the new platform-based naming.** Just like every company wanted "[Name]Platform.com" in 2020, now they'll want "[Function]Agent.com"

2. **Industry-specific AI tooling is where the money will be.** Generic AI domains are played out, but "LegalAgent," "HRAgent," "ComplianceAgent" - those are businesses waiting to be built.

3. **Infrastructure and operations terminology is heating up.** As AI moves from experimental to operational, look for ops-focused naming patterns: "AIInfra," "ModelOps," "AgentOps"

**12-24 month predictions:**
- Agent-based terminology will be as common as "platform" terminology is today
- We'll see a wave of industry-specific AI agent companies, each needing authoritative domain names
- Infrastructure and operations tooling for AI will become its own category (think DataDog for AI agents)
